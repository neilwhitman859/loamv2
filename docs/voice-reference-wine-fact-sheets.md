# Voice Reference: The Anatomy of a Great Wine Fact Sheet

> **Context for Loam:** This document captures a sensibility, not a rulebook. The producers who write great fact sheets speak from the heart with genuine knowledge and love for their subject. That's what Loam should do too — knowledgeable but unpretentious, specific but not clinical, genuinely interested in *why* a place or wine matters. Not a template to follow rigidly. Just a north star for voice.

-----

**The best wine tech sheets are not data sheets — they are arguments for a wine's reason to exist.** After analyzing 50+ actual fact sheets from producers across Napa Valley, Bordeaux, Burgundy, the Rhône, Italy, Spain, Oregon, Washington, and Australia, a clear pattern emerges: the memorable ones share a common architecture but diverge wildly in voice, and it is voice — not data — that separates the forgettable from the collectible. For a wine called Loam, whose very name evokes soil and place, these findings offer a precise blueprint for writing something authentic, distinctive, and genuinely compelling.

The Cain Five fact sheet you're modeling after sits at the pinnacle of this form. Christopher Howell's writing voice — philosophical, humble, conversational, radically place-centered — represents the "winemaker-auteur" style that the most discerning collectors and trade professionals respond to. Understanding what makes it work requires understanding the full spectrum of what exists.

-----

## Every great fact sheet contains seven elements in roughly the same order

Across all 50+ sheets analyzed, the sections that appear and their typical sequencing follow a remarkably consistent grammar, though the best producers rearrange it to express their identity.

**The universal architecture is:** header/identity → vineyard/source → varietal composition → vintage narrative → winemaking notes → tasting description → technical data. Within this framework, producers signal their priorities by what they lead with. Opus One leads with brand philosophy ("the realized dream of two men"). Stag's Leap CASK 23 leads with historical narrative (the 1974 discovery by André Tchelistcheff). Ridge Monte Bello leads with blend and scores. Domaine Drouhin Oregon leads with Burgundian heritage. Christopher Howell's Cain Five leads with harvest and varietal data, then immediately pivots to prose — a structural choice that says: *here are the facts, now let me tell you what they mean.*

The **seven core sections**, ranked by how universally they appear:

1. **Varietal composition with percentages** — present on 100% of sheets analyzed. This is the one non-negotiable. Even Château Margaux, whose official fact sheet is barely 100 words, includes vine stock breakdown.
2. **Oak/aging details** — present on roughly 95% of sheets. The specificity varies enormously: Duckhorn says "100% French Oak | 50% New Oak | 16 months." Joseph Phelps names individual cooperage houses (Taransaud, Demptos, Marcel Cadet). Shafer specifies French forest origins (Allier & Tronçais). Ridge uses primarily American oak — an identity-defining choice at this tier.
3. **Tasting notes or wine description** — present on roughly 90% of sheets. The notable exceptions are Sassicaia, whose official tech sheet contains zero tasting notes (the wine is described only through its making), and Château Margaux's official fact sheet, which omits tasting entirely. This inverse relationship between prestige and verbosity is worth noting: **the greater the wine, the less it needs to explain itself.**
4. **Vintage/growing season narrative** — present on roughly 85% of sheets. This section is where the best writing lives and where most producers differentiate themselves.
5. **Vineyard and terroir information** — present on roughly 80% of sheets. The depth ranges from Stag's Leap naming specific vineyard blocks (2B, 2A, 3B of S.L.V.) to Caymus simply noting "8 of Napa's 16 sub-appellations."
6. **Production numbers and release date** — present on roughly 60% of sheets. Cain Five includes both (5,447 cases; September 1, 2017). Many prestige estates omit case counts.
7. **Analytical chemistry (ABV, pH, TA)** — present on roughly 50% of sheets. Ridge includes everything (ABV, pH, TA, Brix, even sulfur ppm and rainfall data). Opus One, Dominus, Joseph Phelps, and nearly every Italian, Spanish, and French producer omit pH and TA entirely. This is the most variable data point and its inclusion signals audience: pH and TA are for sommeliers and the trade; their absence signals a consumer-facing document.

One additional element appears on the most distinctive sheets: **a personal sign-off**. Howell signs as "Christopher Howell, Wine-Grower." Shafer quotes and attributes winemaker Elias Fernandez by name. Ridge's winemakers initial their notes with dates (TG 4/23). Domaine Drouhin ends with "Santé!" These signatures transform a product document into a personal letter.

## Design principles that signal quality without shouting

Format communicates philosophy before a single word is read. The 50+ sheets analyzed fall along a clear spectrum, and the position a producer occupies on this spectrum tells you everything about their brand.

**The minimalist pole** is occupied by Opus One (ultra-clean, large photography, expandable sections, no scores or press quotes), Château Margaux (~100 words total, no tasting notes), and Vega Sicilia (which barely publishes tech sheets at all). These producers weaponize negative space. What they withhold — ABV, pH, production numbers, food pairings — reinforces their luxury positioning. The wine needs no explanation.

**The maximalist pole** is occupied by Ridge Monte Bello (includes Brix, sulfur ppm at crush and during aging, rainfall data, bloom date, winemaker initials on every note, and a full ingredient disclosure) and Stag's Leap CASK 23 (harvest dates, ABV, pH, TA, fermentation percentages, barrel details, cases, release date, winemaker name, food pairing, and block-level vineyard sourcing). These producers weaponize transparency. What they include — everything — is itself a philosophical statement.

**The Cain Five occupies the sweet spot**: enough data to satisfy (blend percentages, production, release date, estate-grown designation) but the majority of the page given to prose. This balanced approach works because the data establishes credibility while the writing establishes identity.

Across all sheets, several format principles recur:

**Prose beats bullet points.** The most memorable sheets use flowing paragraphs, not data tables. Cristom's tagline format ("LAND | STEWARDSHIP | CRAFTSMANSHIP") is an exception that works because it's a three-word manifesto, not a spec list. L'Ecole No 41's bullet-point vintage notes feel clinical by comparison.

**One page is the discipline.** Nearly every premium tech sheet fits on a single page. This constraint forces economy — every word earns its place. As Charlie Geoghegan advises: "Whether you write 10 words or 1,000, make sure that each is there for a reason."

**The label image anchors the visual.** Most sheets include a label replica or bottle shot, typically at upper left or as a background element. This grounds the document as a physical product artifact.

**Headers function as section breaks, not decoration.** The Cain Five uses question-form headers — "How did we make this wine?" and "What's in this wine?" — that are conversational and direct. Beaucastel uses "THE WINE," "THE VINTAGE," "THE TERROIR." Ridge uses single-word functional headers. Ornellaia uses none at all, letting the prose flow. The choice communicates formality level.

**White space signals confidence.** Dense, information-packed sheets (like Duckhorn's trade sheet with UPC codes, SRP, and format availability) read as commercial. Generous white space reads as premium. The Cain Five's "That's it. We try to keep it simple" — followed by actual white space — is perhaps the most elegant format choice in any tech sheet analyzed.

## The winemaker's voice falls into six distinct modes

The most revealing finding from this analysis is that wine tech sheet writing is not one genre — it is at least six, and the choice of voice is the single most consequential decision a fact sheet writer makes.

**The Winemaker-Auteur voice** (first person singular/plural, philosophical, personal): This is Christopher Howell's mode, and it is the rarest and most powerful. Howell writes: *"Despite our constant commitment to balance and grace, despite our long demonstrated belief that power and intensity do not equate to quality, the 2013 Cain Five is undoubtedly the most dense and structured Cain Five since the 1994."* The opening subordinate clause — "despite our constant commitment" — establishes decades of philosophy before introducing the specific wine. His 2016 factsheet contains perhaps the single best line on any tech sheet ever written: **"The Cain Five tastes like nothing else — not because of us but because of where it grows."** This radical deflection of credit from winemaker to place is his signature move. He signs as "Wine-Grower," not "Winemaker." He quotes Simone Weil on attention and prayer. He writes *"Rain began falling on the 14th of October. Yes, we really did pick before the rain"* — a conversational aside that no corporate writer would include but that lands perfectly because it is unmistakably human.

**The Team Narrative voice** (first person plural, warm but institutional): The most common voice among premium producers. Ridge writes: *"It is our practice to let this unique vineyard site create the wine rather than try to steer it toward a particular style in the cellar."* Tablas Creek: *"A little kiss of oak."* Spottswoode: *"Our lanky thoroughbred is getting ready to run."* This voice works because the "we" is strategically inclusive — it implies a team, a tradition, and continuity, while remaining human enough to include personality. The best practitioners (Ridge, Tablas Creek) inject genuine character; the worst lapse into corporate warmth.

**The Poetic-Narrative voice** (literary, often third person, culturally rich): This is the French mode at its finest. Château Lynch-Bages gives every vintage a cultural title — "Blue Velvet…" (2019), "Purple Rain…" (2018), "Fiat Lux!" (2020), "Nature acts, man creates" (2023). Pontet-Canet reaches for textile metaphors: *"The velvet texture no longer describes the tannins but more that of lace."* Ornellaia frames wine as philosophy: *"Complex ancient soils converse through human wisdom with a delicate microclimate."* Bergström writes love letters to soil: *"most probably is a 60-million-year-old sand dune or beach."* Antica Terra's Maggie Harrison pushes into synaesthesia: *"Intensely mineral, it bears, somehow, a seemingly synesthetic resemblance to the ground from which it was born."*

**The Radical Transparency voice** (mixed voice, ingredient-focused, anti-marketing): Ridge is the exemplar. Their back label reads: *"Hand-harvested; estate grown Monte Bello Vineyard grapes, destemmed and sorted; fermented on the native yeasts; full malolactic on the naturally occurring bacteria; oak from barrel aging; minimum effective sulfur 35 ppm at crush, 108 ppm during aging; depth filtered at bottling. In keeping with our philosophy of minimal intervention, this is the sum of our actions."* The final sentence — "this is the sum of our actions" — transforms an ingredient list into a philosophical manifesto. No other producer at this tier discloses sulfur ppm levels. This is transparency as brand identity.

**The Theatrical-Personal voice** (first person, performative, exuberant): Penfolds' Peter Gago is the master: *"An instant cornucopia of Grange markers — where to begin?! This 2021 vintage is certainly expansive across the palate."* His tasting notes include exclamation marks, rhetorical questions ("Or is it?!"), and sensory descriptions that read like culinary avant-garde: *"cold corned beef with peppercorn? Maybe, from Barossa — soaked dark satsuma plum."* This voice is boldly, unapologetically Australian. It would be catastrophic on a Bordeaux tech sheet and perfect for Grange.

**The Restrained-Factual voice** (third person, passive, minimal): Château Margaux's official fact sheet: *"Red vines: 215 acres of appellation contrôlée of which 202 acres planted."* Sassicaia describes winemaking entirely in passive voice: *"Very soft pressing to avoid breaking the grapes and delicate destemming to avoid the release of improper tannins."* Guigal's website: *"Eye: Dark ruby red. Nose: Spices, red berries and delicate oak aromas."* This voice works only when backed by centuries of reputation. For everyone else, it reads as generic.

## What separates the memorable from the forgettable

The difference between a tech sheet that gets filed and one that gets remembered comes down to five specific qualities, all of which the Cain Five exemplifies and most producers fail at.

**Specificity over generality.** Howell writes: *"The soils here are almost all uniformly thin and poor, derived from sandstone, shale, and clay. The vines are small, even scrawny, and the clusters are small and the berries are tiny."* Generic version: "Our mountain vineyard benefits from well-drained rocky soils." Matthiasson describes *"a foot of silty loam sitting on top of at least 20 feet of pure cobble."* Chapoutier names each parcel's geological origin: *"les Bessards: from a granitic origin, it constitutes the 'soul' of a good Hermitage."* The specific detail is always more persuasive than the vague superlative.

**Conversational honesty over promotional polish.** Howell: *"I don't think that we'll ever 'figure out' how to make Cain Five. Each vintage is a process of discovery."* Stolpman: *"We only pray for healthy winter rains during dormancy so the vines wake up sensing enough moisture to get through the summer."* Bedrock: *"If something has been growing for over 100 years it not only deserves the respect of us humans, but it probably makes some darn good wine too."* The willingness to sound human — to use "scrawny" instead of "elegant," "pray" instead of "manage," "darn good" instead of "exceptional" — is what separates authentic from corporate.

**Philosophy stated, not implied.** The best tech sheets contain at least one sentence that articulates a winemaking worldview. Howell: *"Despite our long demonstrated belief that power and intensity do not equate to quality."* Ridge: *"In keeping with our philosophy of minimal intervention, this is the sum of our actions."* Clonakilla: *"Our task as winemakers is simple: capture what is good and true in the landscape and bottle it for people to enjoy."* Eyrie's Jason Lett: *"'What are you going to change?' My answer was, 'Fundamentally? Nothing.'"* These declarative philosophical statements give the reader a framework for understanding every other detail on the page.

**Place described as character, not setting.** Howell: *"Just to stand on our mountain and to inhale the scented air reminds one that this place is like no other — and you can taste it in the wine."* He notes the surrounding trees — "oak, madrone, bay laurel, and Douglas fir" — and suspects they contribute to the wine's aromatic signature. Bergström describes a vineyard as *"Reminiscent of a terracotta, scorched earth amphitheater."* Clos Mogador: *"like an immense licorella amphitheater, bathed at its feet by the beautiful Moorish stories of the Siurana River."* When place is a character with texture and story, the wine inherits that narrative depth.

**Sensory language rooted in landscape, not in wine-cliché.** The generic tech sheet says "aromas of dark fruit, vanilla, and spice." Howell says *"the herbal 'garrigue' of the Cain Vineyard comes truffles, a bit of earthiness, and some lovely floral notes of roses and jasmine."* Tablas Creek says *"sun-warmed chaparral."* Torbreck says *"satsuma plum, dried rose petals, Turkish delight, earl grey tea."* The best tasting notes pull their vocabulary from the landscape around the vineyard and from specific sensory memories, not from the standard wheel of wine descriptors. Howell's palette consistently leans toward secondary and tertiary descriptors — earth, herbs, flowers, garrigue — rather than primary fruit, which mirrors his philosophy that wines should express place, not varietal character.

## How winemakers write about terroir, vintage, and philosophy reveals cultural fault lines

**Terroir description follows a clear cultural gradient.** American producers describe terroir geographically: "west-facing slopes of the Sonoma Coast." French producers describe it geologically: Beaucastel's *"Molasse seabed of the Miocene period covered by diluvial alpine deposits (rolled pebbles)."* Italian producers describe it through classification systems: "Bolgheri DOC Sassicaia." Spanish producers describe it mystically: Álvaro Palacios's *"broken, open soils comprised of the famed 'Llicorella' slate"* — where "famed" signals cultural pride in stone. Australian producers describe it through vine heritage: Henschke's *"vines originate from pre-phylloxera material brought from Europe by the early European settlers… Mass selection carried out over three generations."*

The artisan American producers who stand out — Howell, Matthiasson, Bergström, Tablas Creek — all borrow from the French geological tradition. They name soil types, parent rock, and geological age. For Loam, this is directly relevant: **the name itself is a soil type, so the tech sheet should treat soil description as the organizing center of the terroir section.**

**Vintage narratives follow three archetypal arcs.** The Triumph: "Despite a challenging growing season…" (near-universal, often formulaic). The Ideal: Howell's *"2016 was one of those years, neither too hot nor too cold, where ripening could proceed smoothly and progressively"* (rarer, more confident). The Elegy: Howell's 2018 factsheet — *"In 2020, in the midst of harvest, the Cain Vineyard was decimated by wildfire, and the winery was lost along with the 2019 and 2020 vintages. The 2018 Cain Five is the last vintage to represent the vineyard as we knew it"* — transforms a product document into a memorial. The most effective vintage narratives personify weather as a character, include specific dates ("Rain began falling on the 14th of October"), and resist the temptation to declare every year exceptional.

**Winemaking philosophy descriptions cluster around a shared lexicon** that has become nearly universal among premium producers: "native yeast fermentations," "unfined and unfiltered," "whole-cluster" with specific percentages, "gravity flow," "minimal intervention," "letting the vineyard speak." The danger is that this language has become so common it risks meaninglessness. The producers who transcend it do so through specificity (Ridge listing sulfur ppm), humor (Stolpman's "knock on wood"), intellectual depth (Howell quoting Simone Weil), or radical simplicity (Cain Five's "That's it. We try to keep it simple").

## Building the Loam fact sheet: what all of this means in practice

The name "Loam" is a gift. It is a soil word — tactile, specific, grounded. It immediately signals a terroir-first philosophy. Based on everything analyzed across these 50+ sheets, here is what the findings suggest for building a fact sheet worthy of that name.

**Lead with data, then earn the prose.** Follow the Cain Five structure: header with contact information, then a clean data block (vineyard source, varietal percentages, production, release date, estate designation), then transition into the extended prose section. This structure says: we respect your time, here are the facts — and now, if you'll stay with us, here is what they mean.

**Write in first person plural with moments of singular.** The "we" voice establishes a team and a tradition. But allow the winemaker's individual perspective to surface in key moments — a parenthetical aside, a philosophical declaration, an observation that could only come from one person standing in one specific place. Howell's toggle between "we" and "I" is the model.

**Describe your soil the way Howell describes his.** Not "well-drained alluvial soils" but the specific composition, the parent rock, what grows in it and next to it. The name Loam practically demands a sentence about what makes your loam different from all other loam. Howell writes about vines that are "small, even scrawny." Chapoutier says granite constitutes "the soul of a good Hermitage." Matthiasson describes "a foot of silty loam sitting on top of at least 20 feet of pure cobble." This specificity is what makes terroir writing believable.

**Include a clear philosophical statement.** One sentence that declares what you believe about wine. Not a slogan — a conviction. Howell's "power and intensity do not equate to quality." Ridge's "this is the sum of our actions." Clonakilla's "our task is simple: capture what is good and true." This sentence becomes the lens through which every other detail is read.

**End with a personal sign-off.** Howell signs as "Wine-Grower." This small choice radiates identity. The sign-off says: a specific human made this wine and stands behind it. It transforms the document from marketing collateral into a personal guarantee.

**Resist the complete tasting note cliché.** Instead of listing twelve flavor descriptors, anchor the sensory description in two or three that are genuinely distinctive and tied to place. "Sun-warmed chaparral" tells you more about a wine than "notes of blackberry, vanilla, and mocha" ever could. For a wine called Loam, the sensory vocabulary should lean toward earth, mineral, and botanical — the secondary and tertiary register that signals complexity and terroir-expression rather than fruit-forward winemaking.

**Include a simple ingredients or winemaking section.** The Cain Five's "What's in this wine?" section is brilliant in its plainness. Ridge's full ingredient disclosure is the gold standard. This kind of transparency is rare enough to be distinctive and signals a producer with nothing to hide. For Loam, a clean, direct statement of what went into the wine — and nothing more — would reinforce the name's earthy honesty.

**Let the closing breathe.** "That's it. We try to keep it simple." Six words that do more work than six paragraphs of marketing copy. The willingness to stop — to leave white space on the page, to let the wine speak for itself — is the ultimate mark of confidence. The most prestigious estates in the world (Margaux, Vega Sicilia, Opus One) communicate through what they withhold. The best artisan producers (Cain, Ridge, Eyrie) communicate through what they state plainly and then step away from.

## Conclusion

The wine fact sheet is a deceptively powerful form. At its worst, it is a forgettable data dump of interchangeable descriptors and passive-voice process notes — what one critic calls documents where "six out of ten times they fall short in providing quality information." At its best, as exemplified by Cain Five, it is a one-page essay that establishes a wine's reason to exist in the world: where it comes from, what it believes, and why it tastes like nothing else. The difference is not design or data selection. **The difference is whether the person writing it has something to say** — about the land, the vintage, and the meaning of making wine — and the discipline to say it plainly. For Loam, the fact sheet should read like the soil it's named for: layered, particular, alive with the specificity of a single place, and honest enough to let that place be the story.
