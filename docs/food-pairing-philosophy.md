# Loam — Food Pairing Philosophy

This guide applies everywhere food pairing content appears: grape insights, wine insights, tasting contexts, any future feature. It's the filter for how Claude writes about food and wine together.

---

## Name real food

Specific dishes people recognize. Not "a delicate preparation of stone fruit and charcuterie" — just "prosciutto and melon." Name the cuisine: Thai, Mexican, Korean, Southern US, Japanese, Ethiopian, Indian, Lebanese. These are searchable, relatable, and honest about how people actually eat.

## Start with the classics

Traditional pairings exist for a reason. Sancerre and goat cheese. Chianti and tomato-based pasta. Riesling and pork. Champagne and fried chicken. Malbec and grilled steak. Lead with these — they're the foundation. Then expand outward to less obvious territory.

## Cover the full table

A Tuesday night meal and a Saturday dinner. Muscadet gets oysters on the half shell *and* fish tacos with lime crema. Barolo gets braised short ribs *and* mushroom pizza with truffle oil. Not every grape needs both ends of the spectrum, but it's preferred where it fits naturally. Let the grape's world guide the balance — don't force casual on a grape that lives in formal contexts, or vice versa.

## Explain the why

The flavor logic matters more than the specific dish. "The high acid cuts through rich, fatty dishes" teaches someone to pair on their own. "The residual sugar tames chili heat" explains why Riesling and Thai food work. Lead with real dishes, but weave in the reasoning so the pairing makes sense.

## Vegetarian pairings appear naturally

Don't force a plant-based option onto every grape. Don't exclude them either. Grüner Veltliner gets asparagus and white bean salad because those are genuinely great pairings, not because we need a checkbox. Nebbiolo gets mushroom risotto because Piedmont runs on mushrooms. Let the grape's food world dictate what shows up.

## No sommelier theater

Banned patterns:
- "pairs beautifully with a delicate..."
- "an exquisite accompaniment to..."
- "elevates the dining experience..."
- Listing only fine-dining dishes (foie gras, Dover sole, beef Wellington)
- Generic cop-outs ("pairs well with grilled meats and seafood")

Just name the food. If it's good with Nashville hot chicken, say Nashville hot chicken.
